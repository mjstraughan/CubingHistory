4. Corner orientation the skewed way...
From: "Stefan Pochmann" <pochmann@...>
To: blindfoldsolving-rubiks-cube@yahoogroups.com
Date: Mon, 12 Jan 2004 21:37:07 -0000

I thought about orienting the ULF, URB, DLB and DRF corners with one algorithm and then the other four corners with another algorithm. That is, assuming those two groups can be oriented independently (each sums up to a multiple of 3). If not, one more preparation alg can make it so. The nice thing is that you need only very few algorithms and before and after applying one of them you only need to turn the cube as a whole, no "real" moves are necessary. There are only four sets of orientations: - { 0, 0, 0, 0 } - { 0, 0, 1, 2 } - { 0, 1, 1, 1 } - { 0, 2, 2, 2 } - { 1, 1, 2, 2 } You can always turn the whole cube so that the smallest of those numbers is at ULF, the second smallest at URB, the third smallest at DLB and the largest at DRF. Then apply one of only four (three without inversion) algorithms. Did someone try this before? Stefan

Here's how I solve the cube blindfolded quickly: First off I see if there will be a parity error. I quickly count off the corner cycles. Corner cycles can be learned on stiff_hands' page if you don't understand them. Anyways after counting the cycles real fast you subtract 1 from each cycle and add the number together and just pray that it's an even number. Otherwise you have a parity error. For me it's easier to get rid of it from the start by just turning U at the beginning, but the only bad thing here is that you have to memorize the cube as if you had already turned U so it takes some mental strain to get it right. I often memorize a rollercoaster going from one corner to the next. If the situation is difficult you can still see what the cycle would look like. I *only* use 3-cycles. Here's how it works: If your cycle is (xyz) then a three-cycle will solve it. To understand what three-cycle permute move you must use, you must think about which piece is where upon setup. X moves to Y which moves to Z which moves to X, so you must know which corner permute will do so. One moves corners clockwise and the other moves them counterclockwise. If your cycle is (vwxyz)or longer, then a three-cycle will reduce it to (vyz). If your cycle has (wx)(yz) in it, then the easiest way for me to do this is to treat the first three corners as one cycle. This means I will cycle (wxy). By doing so, (wx)(yz) reduces to (wzy). The second corner of the first cycle drops out, while the two corners in the second cycle switch places, and both of these initial cycles become one. Or, you can use a four-corner swap move. I've just found this way to be easy and consistent and you don't need to memorize more algorithms if you have not already. To each their own. As for edge orientation, this is really easy and can be memorized with a mental picture more often than not because often there is not much to orient, in my experiences. At most, you have to memorize 6 edges. Anything more and you're working too hard. If there are say, 8 edges to orient, just memorize the 4 edges that are already oriented and screw those up, then do a Superflip move which can be found on many sites. You memorize far less on this step than anything else so it should be fast. To tell if a piece is oriented or not: First, mind you that at this point you can do *any* move you want, but if you are turning L or R, it MUST be L2 and R2. No L, no L', no R', no R. The only exception to this rule is the actual permuting moves themselves. But in terms of setup, never do single turns of L or R. Anyhow, you must think *if I were to move this edge around the cube any which way, would I be able to line its colors up with the centers?* For instance, let's say an edge was blue and red. If you brute-forced it into that position by using any moves (between the red and blue centers) would it line up nicely (blue center, blue edge, red edge, red center) or not (blue center, red edge, blue edge, red center). Edge permutation takes the longest to memorize and is the hardest to do correctly. Taking a mental picture here is a bit hard but still works but memorizing number cycles here may be a bit easier. I do whatever moves will get the three edges I need to the U layer, then cycle them, then undo my setup move. Again, make sure your setup moves do not involve single turns of L or R. Also, three-cycling on any other face (except D) will mess up your orientation. If you can help it, keep it to the U face. The cycles work the same way as corners in terms of how they are affected (xyz), (vwxyz), or (wx)(yz). Eh hope this helps :P

That lengthy intro aside, this is the first step to my "simplification" - how I go about corner orientation (CO). I memorize orientations (for both corners and edges) as outlined in the aforementioned .pdf document (available at http://www.ws.binghamton.edu/fridrich/Richard/BlindfoldRevenge.pdf ), though I find it easier to remember as a sequence of 4 numbers (e.g. 12, 11, 1, 21), instead of a string of 8. To do CO, first, orient as much of the F face as you can - in the example sequence I gave, UFR and DFL would need a clockwise (C) twist, and UFL would require a counterclockwise (CC) twist. Therefore, I can twist UFL CC, and either UFR or DFL C, leaving the other still needing a C twist. To twist a given corner: - Move it to UFR with a twist of F - Perform (R' U R U')*2 to twist it CC or (U R' U' R)*2 to twist it C - Move it back to its original position with a twist of F Notice that you handle each piece individually, so there's only one setup move to remember - though for speed you can compress successive turns of F, just putting the corners into UFR one after another, until all (except maybe 1) are complete. As long as you twist 1 or 2 pairs of corners in opposite directions or exactly 3 in the same direction, it doesn't matter what order you do them in. Next, do the same thing with the B face. Again, in my example sequence, all B corners require a C twist, except DBR, which requires a CC twist. To twist a given back corner: - Move it to UBR with a twist of B - Perform (R U' R' U)*2 to twist it C or (U' R U R')*2 to twist it CC - Move it back to its original position with a twist of B Again, you just need to keep track of which corner you are orienting, and you know what setup move you need to reverse. Once this is complete, there will be at most 2 corners that still need to be oriented - one on F and one on B. For ease of remembering the setup moves, always do the same thing to bring the two corners into the same face: - Move B until the corners are diagonally opposite on the puzzle (such that they share no faces) - Do L2 or R2 to bring the B corner to F - Orient the corners as you would normally do on the F face - Reverse the L or R turn - Reverse the B turn I like this approach because it uses as most 2 setup moves for any situation and doesn't require any cube rotations. Also, the algs are short and easy (nicely triggered). What do yo think? - Grant

In other words, there must be at least 2 corners not oriented, and at least 2 edges not oriented. My idea is too keep it simple and lenient so we can have fun making fast times, at the same time not too lenient as to have ridiculous cases. From: "Grant Tregay" <Grant@...>

--- brokulo wrote: > (UFL UFR)(UBR DBR) > what do you do when you get this? I can do it in 2 different ways > both requiring too many setup moves :&#92; I would do: 1) Permute UFL -> UFR -> UBR with (R B') (R F2) (R' B R) F2 R2 2) Permute UBR -> UFL -> DBR with [(R'B R B')*3 U2]*2 An explanation of this makes for a good transition into the next phase of the "simple" approach to blindfold cubing that I've been working on. It's not actually the second step (after orient corners, I orient edges), but it's the next step that I've got down solidly. For this corner permutation approach, you need to know algs to permute corners on U - these are mine: UFL -> UFR -> UBR = (R B') (R F2) (R' B R) F2 R2 UFL -> UBR -> UFR = (R2 F2) (R' B') (R F2) (R' B R') UFL <-> UBL UFR <-> UBR = F (R B) (R' F' R) (L F) (L' B' L) F' L' R' UFL <-> UBR UFR <-> UBL = (R L) U2 (R' L') (F' B') U2 (F B) U2 Technically it's not necessary to know algs for these, but it's much quicker if you know them, and most cubists do, so it's not a tough requirement. Just remember that the last one is the same as swapping opposite edges (UF <-> UB UL <->UR) with an extra U2 twist at the beginning or end. In addition to knowing those algs, there are two others (very simple and quick): Alg1 - UBR <-> DBR UFR <-> UBL = (R B' R' B) * 3 Alg2 - UBR <-> DBR DFR <-> DBL = (R' B R B') * 3 Again... This approach is based on the idea of minimizing setup moves and necessary algorithms - by no means is this efficient in number of moves, but it is very comfortable to perform (at least in my opinion) and setup moves are logical and minimal. Here's how it works... First, comes the memorization and manipulation of the state during solving. Memorize the corners' using a cycle method - I apply the numbers 1-8 to UFR, UFL, UBR, UBL, DFR, DFL, DBR and DBL respectively. Therefore, the cycle UFR -> UFL -> UBR would be 1->2->3 in my system, but the numbers you use are up to you. Once you have the corner permutation cycles all memorized, go through the cycles in order, sequentially solving and removing them from the list. If you have a cycle that is 3 or longer, cycle the first 3, and remove the second and third from the cycle (e.g. 1->2->3->4->5 becomes 1->4->5). If it was exactly a cycle of three, it becomes a cycle of 1 (solved) and can be dropped. If the cycle is only 2, cycle them in order with the first from the next cycle. Then, drop the second number from the 2 cycle and insert the first one after the first number in the second cycle. For example, if you need to swap (UFL <-> UFR)(UBR <-> DBR) as suggested in the original post, cycle UFL->UFR->UBR, and you'll be left with a 3 cycle of UBR->UFL->DBR (UFR becomes solved, and UFL gets inserted in the second cycle after UBR). That's the mental game involved - now on to the actual application. If you need to cycle 3 corners or swap 2 pairs of corners on U, use a known alg. Do the same for D, with a cube rotation before and after as setup and reversal. When you want to cycle three corners where two are on U and the other on D, or vice versa, do this: 1) Move the lone piece to UBR or DBR with a turn of the U or D face 2) Move the "first" of the other two corners (the corner that stays on U or stays on D) directly above/below the lone piece with a turn of U or D 3) Perform Alg1 (2 corners on D) or Alg2 (2 corners on U) 4) Turn U or D to move the last corner directly above/below the lone piece 5) Perform the same alg as in #4 6) Put U and D back to original rotation Here's an example... Let's say you want to rotate UFR -> UBL -> DFL. Here are the steps as outlined above: 1) Turn DFL to DBR with D2 2) Turn UFR to UBR with U' 3) Perform Alg2, which is (R' B R B') * 3 4) Turn UBL to UBR with U2 (reverse U' with U, then add U to put UBL at UBR - total turn is 1/2) 5) Perform Alg2 - still (R' B R B') * 3 6) Turn U' and D2 to return U and D faces to original states. If you don't know algs to permute corners on U, and don't want to learn them, you can just turn a face to put 2 on U and 1 on D and then follow the same procedure... If you get to the point that you have just one 2 cycle left, use your favorite PLL alg that swaps one corner pair and one edge pair. If you don't know one, learning one is better than the alternative, which would be to do U or U', adjust your mental picture of edge and corner permutations, and finish it off from there... I've been looking a lot into memorizing and solving orders for 3x3 blindfolded solving, and I think I've come up with a pretty good order. I used to memorize EO CO EP CP then solve EO CO EP CP Then I switched to memorizing as CP CO EP EO then solving as EO EP CO CP and my average dropped by a couple minutes on average. I posted about this order a while ago, and how I thought it was an improvement over my first order. Just recently I switched to memorizing as EP EO CP CO then solving as CO CP EO EP and my average has again dropped by a couple minutes on average. Here's my thinking for why this new order works well. The edges are clearly the hardest part of a blindfolded solve (orientation and permutation) since there are 12 pieces instead of 8. So my strategy now is to memorize them first, so that I have more time to try to plan what I will do for the edges while doing other steps. While memorizing the edge cycles, I'm trying to decode the first one or two permutations that I will do. That's about even and odd permutations, but what it comes down to just this: In general, you can not solve corners and edges independently. Most people are trying to do exactly that (i.e. first solving corners without changing edges, then solving edges without changing corners, or vice versa), but in 50% of all cases it's just not possible. For example, solve the cube and then do a single U turn. Now try to solve all corners without changing the edges. It's not possible. Best you can do is solve all but 2 corners. You can then solve all but 2 edges, not changing the corners further. So at the end, you still need to swap two corners and two edges. There are also other ways of dealing with this issue, of course... Cheers!
